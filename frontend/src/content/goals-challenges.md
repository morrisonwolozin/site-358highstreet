# Renovation Goals and Challenges

_358 High Street, Belfast, ME_

The project's goals were to create energy-efficient and healthy dwelling units while eliminating all fossil fuel combustion equipment. 

## Overall Goals:

1. Good indoor air quality, continuously monitored;
2. High performing air and sound isolation between units;
3. Low cooling and heating demand and energy consumption;
4. Improved livability by adding half baths, laundry spaces, mudrooms, conditioned and unconditioned storage;

## Retrofit Scope of Work:

1. Excavate original building foundation perimeter to apply water-proofing, replace drainage, add drainage from roof;
2. Site grading to improve surface water diversion and increase parking;
3. Added conditioned space for each unit inlcuding an entry with laundry alcove and conditioned storage;
4. Replaced original party wall with insulated double wall construction for sound attenuation;
5. Added  interior half baths in place of the original shared laundry room and closets;
6. Added ed insulation to walls, roof and air seal all spaces, "deep energy retrofit lite";
7. Heat pump domestic water heater for each unit;
8. Ducted central heat pump heating and cooling for each unit;
9. Variable speed kitchen range exhaust hoods;
10. Energy recovery ventilator (ERV) for each unit;
11. New kitchens and appliances;
12. New bathrooms and fixtures;
13. Added ceiling lighting for all spaces, including closets, removed switched recepticles;
14. Added crawl space floor insulation, "dimple mat", subfloor;
15. Added an insulated sound and fire wall between units in the crawlspace;
16. On site unconditioned storage and garden beds.

The following pre-construction conditions defined the scope of the retrofit.

## Challenges
### 1. Crawl space flooding
Poor site water management and unmanaged roof runoff caused the crawl space to flood after major rain events. A December 2023 storm left an inch of standing water that took over 24 hours to drain, with residual puddles persisting for days. This water intrusion contributed to chronically elevated indoor humidity (roughly 20% above outdoor air), acceptable in winter but excessive in summer and shoulder seasons, and a long-term mold risk.
### 2. Aging oil-fired boiler plant
The boiler was functioning but at end of economic life. Its crawl space location beneath Unit 1 made maintenance difficult. The induced draft fan discharge was insufficiently elevated (noted in the sales inspection), and operational noise was excessive. Fuel oil tubing ran along the crawl space floor — a leak would go unnoticed until significant, and in combination with flooding, posed a site contamination risk.
### 3. Uneven electric consumption allocation
With two tenant services and no landlord meter, shared loads — clothes dryer, boiler, washing machine — were assigned to one unit's service, making equal cost-sharing impossible.
### 4. Incomplete lighting
Bedrooms and living rooms had no built-in lighting; switched receptacles were provided which requiring plug-in fixtures.
### 5. No site lighting
No exterior lighting of any kind.
### 6. Insufficient Ventilation Air
Air leakage testing measured 3.68 ACH50 — approximately one natural air change every five hours — confirming that infiltration alone was insufficient for acceptable ventilation. AirThings monitors confirmed poor air quality in both units.
### 7. No fire nor sound separation between units in crawlspace
The original crawl space was open between units, providing no barrier to fire spread, sound transmission, or air movement.
### 8. Excessive inter-unit air leakage
There was significant air transfer between units. CO₂ readings in unoccupied Unit 1 were elevated, and painting odors from Unit 1 entered Unit 2.
### 9. Other Limitations
One bathroom per unit, Unit 2 had no dishwasher, Unit 1's refrigerator had failed, and two range burners did not operate.
